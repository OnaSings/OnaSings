How To Do Simple Statistics

One thing I could never really figure out until I was midway through my PhD was how to properly do statistics on my data. Here I am sharing some tips that I found useful to follow. Disclaimer: this workflow by any means is not extensive, I wrote it for myself, to gather the knowledge I already felt I have. This is meant to help undergraduate level bioscience students to wrangle different tests and statistics. If you have good suggestions on what to add to this page, please contact me.

What are you counting?

What data is your dependent variable? Categorical (Nominal vs Ordinal?) or Numerical (Discrete vs Continuous)?
Let us choose numerical. It can be a continuous (ratio) data, such age, height, weight, where you can have parts of a whole, such as 0.5 cm. Or it can be a discrete count, such as a number of somatic cells in a stem cell niche, or a number of students attending professor’s yearly seminar. You can’t possibly have 1/2 of a student.
Let us choose discrete data, such as number of cells. What is your distribution? If your sample size is large enough (n \(\gt\) 30), you can assume normal distribution. If not, plot your data on a QQplot and see if it is bell-shaped.
You might want to throw in a normality test such as Shapiro-Wilk’s test. Important!: Shapiro-Wilk’s test assumes distribution is normal for p \(\gt\) 0.05. Meaning, if you get a p \(\leqslant\) 0.05, your data is not distributed normally.
